## Macroeconomic & Monetary Events
* FED Quantitative Easing/Tightening: Adjustments to the central bank's balance sheet that increase or decrease the total money supply.
* Yield Curve Inversions: Tracking the spread between long-term and short-term interest rates as a primary recession signal.
* Inflation Trends: The long-term rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services is rising.
* The Velocity of Money: Measuring how fast money circulates through the economy to indicate real economic health versus stagnation.
* Currency De-pegging: When a nation is forced to abandon a fixed exchange rate, usually leading to massive market volatility.
* Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs): The structural move toward digitized government currencies that could bypass traditional banking systems.

## Government & Geopolitical Events
* Fiscal Spending & "Big Bills": Massive government legislative plans that inject capital into specific infrastructure or social sectors.
* Debt-to-GDP Ratios: Identifying "fiscal dominance" thresholds where debt interest payments begin to crowd out all other spending.
* Treaties and Commerce Agreements: Formal pacts between nations that redefine trade barriers and economic cooperation.
* Wars and Conflicts: Military engagements that disrupt global trade routes, resource availability, and defense spending.

* Supply Chain "Friend-shoring": The intentional movement of manufacturing to politically aligned countries to reduce geopolitical risk.
* Resource Nationalization: Countries seizing control of internal assets like lithium or oil from foreign corporations to secure domestic supply.
* Government gridlock: When a country's legislature is unable to pass legislation, leading to economic uncertainty, e.g; France's recent political gridlock (2025).

## Social & Structural Shifts       
* Demographic Changes: Long-term shifts in population age and growth that dictate labor availability and consumer demand.
* Labor Force Participation Rates: Changes in the percentage of the population actively working, which dictates long-term wage inflation.
* Polarization and Decoupling: The social and economic separation of nations or internal factions that breaks down global integration.
* Wealth Concentration Ratios: Tracking the gap between asset owners and wage earners as a predictor of social instability.
* Energy Transition Milestones: Shifts in global subsidies and infrastructure moving from fossil fuels to renewables or nuclear.
* Agricultural Disruption: Long-term changes in water rights and soil viability in major global breadbasket regions.

## Market Valuation & Asset Indicators
* The Buffett Indicator: Monitoring the ratio of total stock market capitalization to a nation's GDP to identify broad overvaluation.
* Total Market Ratios (PE, PEG, P/B): Aggregated valuation metrics used to determine if an entire index is priced high or low relative to its fundamentals.
* Global Market Index Comparisons: Analyzing the relative strength and performance of different regions like the US, China, Europe, and Japan.
* Gold Price Fluctuations: Tracking gold as a long-term barometer for currency debasement and systemic fear.
* Strategic Reserve Volatility: Significant changes in national stockpiles of petroleum or grain that signal future supply shocks.
* Antitrust and Regulatory Shifts: Major court rulings or legislative changes that break up corporate monopolies or alter industry liability.
